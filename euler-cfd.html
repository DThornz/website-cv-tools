Background & Mathematical Foundation

1. Lagrangian vs. Eulerian Description

Any continuous fluid flow can be described in two complementary ways. In the Lagrangian (material) description, we tag and follow individual fluid parcels as they move — recording what happens to specific material. In the Eulerian (field) description, we ask what is the velocity, pressure, or density at a fixed point in space at each instant. The bridge between them is the Fundamental Principle of Kinematics: the Eulerian velocity at position x equals the velocity of whatever parcel happens to occupy that point at that instant (Price, 2023). This simulator is entirely Eulerian — the state vector $\mathbf{U}$ is defined on a fixed rectangular grid, and conservation laws are applied to fixed control volumes rather than moving material volumes.

2. The 2D Compressible Euler Equations

Neglecting viscosity (perfect fluid) and body forces, conservation of mass, x-momentum, y-momentum, and energy on a fixed control volume gives the 2D Euler equations in conservation form:

State + Flux PDE
$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{U}}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{F}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{G}}{\partial y} = 0$$

The conserved state vector $\mathbf{U}$, x-flux $\mathbf{F}$, and y-flux $\mathbf{G}$ are:

State Vector
$$\mathbf{U} = \begin{pmatrix} \rho \\ \rho u \\ \rho v \\ \rho E \end{pmatrix}$$
x-Flux F
$$\mathbf{F} = \begin{pmatrix} \rho u \\ \rho u^2 + p \\ \rho u v \\ u(\rho E + p) \end{pmatrix}$$
y-Flux G
$$\mathbf{G} = \begin{pmatrix} \rho v \\ \rho u v \\ \rho v^2 + p \\ v(\rho E + p) \end{pmatrix}$$

Here $\rho$ is density, $u$ and $v$ are x- and y-velocity components, $p$ is static pressure, and $E$ is specific total energy (kinetic + internal). The variables $\rho u$, $\rho v$ are momentum densities and $\rho E$ is total energy per unit volume.

3. Equation of State (Ideal Gas)

The system is closed with the calorically perfect (ideal) gas relation linking pressure, density, and specific total energy. The pressure and local speed of sound are recovered from the conserved state via:

Pressure
$$p = (\gamma - 1)\!\left(\rho E - \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(u^2+v^2)\right)$$
Speed of Sound
$$c = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma\, p}{\rho}}, \quad \gamma = 1.4 \text{ (air)}$$

4. Non-dimensionalization

All simulation quantities are non-dimensional. Physical units are recovered on the fly for display. Reference scales chosen at sea-level conditions:

In non-dimensional form the reference state is $\tilde{\rho}=1$, $\tilde{p}=1$, $\tilde{c}=\sqrt{\gamma}\approx1.183$. Inlet Mach $M$ sets $\tilde{u}=M$. Physical velocity = $\tilde{|v|}\cdot c_0$; physical pressure = $\tilde{p}\cdot p_0$.

6. Finite-Volume Discretization

The domain is divided into $N_x \times N_y$ uniform rectangular cells of size $\Delta x \times \Delta y$ (square here, $\Delta x = \Delta y$). Integrating the conservation law over cell $(i,j)$ and applying the divergence theorem converts the PDE to an exact flux balance for each cell:

Cell Update (finite volume)
$$\mathbf{U}_{i,j}^{n+1} = \mathbf{U}_{i,j}^{n} - \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x}\!\left(\hat{\mathbf{F}}_{i+\frac{1}{2},j} - \hat{\mathbf{F}}_{i-\frac{1}{2},j}\right) - \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta y}\!\left(\hat{\mathbf{G}}_{i,j+\frac{1}{2}} - \hat{\mathbf{G}}_{i,j-\frac{1}{2}}\right)$$

The interface numerical fluxes $\hat{\mathbf{F}}$, $\hat{\mathbf{G}}$ must be chosen to be consistent and stable — this is the job of the Riemann solver.

7. Rusanov (Local Lax–Friedrichs) Flux

The Rusanov flux (Rusanov, 1961) is a first-order, upwind-biased Riemann solver. At each cell interface separating left state $\mathbf{U}_L$ and right state $\mathbf{U}_R$, it computes a stabilized average flux:

Rusanov Numerical Flux
$$\hat{\mathbf{F}}_{i+\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{2}\!\left[\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{U}_L) + \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{U}_R)\right] - \frac{\lambda_{i+\frac{1}{2}}}{2}\!\left(\mathbf{U}_R - \mathbf{U}_L\right)$$

The dissipation coefficient $\lambda_{i+\frac{1}{2}}$ is the maximum local wave speed at that interface:

Local Wave Speed Estimate
$$\lambda_{i+\frac{1}{2}} = \max\!\left(|u_L| + c_L,\; |u_R| + c_R\right)$$

The first term is the central flux (averaged physical fluxes); the second term is the Rusanov diffusion that damps unphysical oscillations. It is the simplest stable Riemann solver and excellent for educational use, though it adds more numerical diffusion than the exact Roe or HLLC solvers.

8. CFL Stability Condition

For explicit time-marching to remain stable, the timestep $\Delta t$ must not allow information to propagate more than one cell per step. This is the Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy (CFL) condition:

Adaptive Timestep (CFL = 0.40)
$$\frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x} = \frac{\text{CFL}}{\displaystyle\max_{i,j}\!\left(|u_{i,j}| + c_{i,j}\right)}$$

The simulator computes this ratio $\Delta t/\Delta x$ globally each step from the current flow field, shown in the stats bar. A CFL of 0.40 provides a comfortable safety margin below the stability limit of 1.0. Note: the original published artifact used $\Delta t = \text{CFL}/\max S$ (missing the $1/\Delta x$ factor), causing effective CFL ≈ $N_x$ times too large and catastrophic instability. This simulator corrects that error.

9. Under-Relaxation

At high Mach numbers or with strong gradients, the Rusanov update can occasionally produce over-shoots. Under-relaxation blends the candidate update with the previous state:

Under-Relaxation (α slider)
$$\mathbf{U}^{n+1} = \alpha\,\mathbf{U}^{*}_{\text{Rusanov}} + (1-\alpha)\,\mathbf{U}^{n}, \quad \alpha \in [0.3,\;1.0]$$

Mathematically this scales all flux changes by $\alpha$ while keeping the same $\Delta t/\Delta x$ ratio. $\alpha=1$ is the pure Rusanov update; $\alpha=0.5$ halves the effective step size for extra stability.

11. Preset Scenarios

  • NACA 0012 Airfoil (4°): Classic symmetric airfoil at 4° angle of attack. Expect stagnation at the leading edge, accelerated low-pressure flow over the upper surface, and pressure recovery at the trailing edge. This is the canonical subsonic inviscid airfoil case — the lift force is proportional to circulation (Kutta–Joukowski). At $M>0.7$ transonic effects begin: a supersonic bubble forms near the upper surface peak and terminates in a weak normal shock.
  • NACA 0012 Airfoil (8°): Same symmetric profile at double the angle of attack. The increased AoA produces stronger upper-surface acceleration, larger pressure coefficient asymmetry, and more pronounced shock formation in the transonic regime. Compare the pressure field side-by-side with 4° to see the AoA effect on $C_p$ distribution.
  • Circular Cylinder: The canonical bluff-body benchmark. Inviscid potential-flow theory predicts the exact pressure distribution $C_p = 1 - 4\sin^2\theta$ and zero drag (d'Alembert's paradox). The Euler solver approximately reproduces this at low Mach but introduces numerical diffusion. Observe stagnation at the leading edge, $C_p\approx 1$, Bernoulli-type acceleration at the widest point, and the symmetric low-pressure wake.
  • Sphere (axisymmetric cross-section): Same circular geometry as the cylinder but conceptually representing the 2D equatorial cross-section of a 3D sphere. The flow field is identical in the Euler 2D sense. In 3D, a sphere has lower drag than a cylinder due to three-dimensional pressure recovery. Use this case as a reference for comparing circular vs. streamlined (NACA) body performance.
  • Square Block: Sharp leading and trailing edges produce strong separated flow, a wide low-pressure wake, and significantly higher pressure drag than the cylinder. The corners shed oblique compression waves at supersonic speeds. Compare pressure drag $\approx (p_\text{front} - p_\text{back})\times\text{area}$ across different obstacle shapes.
  • Backward-Facing Step: Classic benchmark geometry for flow separation and reattachment. Flow separates at the step corner, forming a recirculation zone (region of reversed $u$), then reattaches on the lower wall downstream. For viscous flow the reattachment length $x_r \approx 6h$–$8h$ (Armaly et al. 1983). The inviscid Euler solver cannot capture the correct viscous reattachment but shows the expansion fan and oblique shock structure at the step corner.
  • Arterial Stenosis (50%): A Gaussian constriction reducing lumen cross-sectional area by ≈50% — representative of an atherosclerotic plaque. Flow accelerates through the throat (Bernoulli: velocity ↑ → pressure ↓), then decelerates in the diffuser region post-stenosis. The post-stenotic pressure drop and the distal low-pressure zone are hemodynamically significant: they contribute to platelet activation and downstream thrombus formation. Clinically, a ≥70% stenosis (area) is generally considered hemodynamically significant. Compare with and without the constriction to see the pressure gradient change.
  • Convergent-Divergent Nozzle (de Laval): The canonical compressible flow device. Flow accelerates in the converging section; at sufficient pressure ratio the throat reaches $M=1$ (choked). In the diverging section, depending on the outlet-to-inlet pressure ratio: (a) subsonic deceleration, (b) design condition (fully supersonic with isentropic expansion), or (c) intermediate — supersonic diverging section with a normal shock at some point inside. Use the Outlet p/p₀ slider to explore these regimes. Observe the Mach number distribution: peak at throat, then rising in the supersonic case.

12. Numerical Limitations & Known Behavior

Understanding what the solver can and cannot do is as important as understanding what it computes:

  • First-order accuracy: The Rusanov scheme is first-order in both space and time. This means flow features are numerically smeared over 2–3 cells. Sharp shocks appear as smooth ramps of width $\sim 2\Delta x$ rather than true discontinuities. Real CFD codes use second-order (MUSCL) or higher-order schemes to reduce this numerical diffusion.
  • No viscosity: The Euler equations have no viscous terms. Boundary layers, flow separation, turbulence, and viscous drag are absent. The d'Alembert paradox (zero drag on a cylinder) is an artifact of this inviscid assumption. Navier-Stokes solvers include the viscous stress tensor $\tau_{ij} = \mu(\partial u_i/\partial x_j + \partial u_j/\partial x_i)$.
  • Steady-state convergence: The simulator advances in (pseudo-)time toward a steady solution. Some geometries (especially bluff bodies at high Mach) produce unsteady vortex shedding even in the inviscid Euler equations — the flow will never fully converge to steady state.
  • Wall boundary artifacts: Interior wall cells are enforced by zeroing momentum — this is a simple "bounce-back" condition that does not enforce a proper no-penetration condition at sub-cell accuracy. Sharp drawn corners may produce unphysical pressure spikes.
  • Supersonic inlet stability: At $M > 1.5$ the inlet condition may conflict with the outlet back-pressure, causing a standing shock to form near the inlet. Lower the under-relaxation α to 0.5–0.6 for stable supersonic simulations.
  • Grid resolution effect: Increasing resolution (more cells) sharpens all features proportionally. A shock that spans 4 cells at Coarse will span $\sim 4$ cells at Ultra — the shock appears sharper in physical space because $\Delta x$ is smaller. Always check if your feature of interest is resolved by ≥3 cells.
